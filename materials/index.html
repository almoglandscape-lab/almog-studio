Materials & planting.

A working glossary of what I keep coming back to. Local stone, native plants, honest metals, and a few principles that hold them together.

Trees

  • Olive (Olea europaea). The anchor of almost every garden — slow, generous, ancient.
  • Carob (Ceratonia siliqua). A wide, calm shade. Drought-proof, long-lived.
  • Palestine oak (Quercus calliprinos). Native canopy, deep roots, quiet authority.
  • Terebinth (Pistacia palaestina). A small, sculptural tree — the colour of old copper in autumn.
  • Judas tree (Cercis siliquastrum). Pink in spring, structural the rest of the year.

Shrubs & aromatics

  • Rosemary (Salvia rosmarinus). Hedge, ground, scent — three jobs in one plant.
  • Lavender (Lavandula dentata, L. stoechas). Mediterranean bee song.
  • Sage (Salvia fruticosa, S. dominica). Native, edible, generous in form.
  • Sumac (Rhus coriaria). Sculptural, autumn-red, drought-wise.
  • Myrtle (Myrtus communis). A green wall that smells of the holidays.

Ground & texture

  • Thyme (Thymus capitatus). A walkable carpet for hot stone.
  • Festuca, Stipa, Pennisetum. Grasses — movement and wind made visible.
  • Sedum and creeping veronica. Roof-light, drought-tough.

Stone

  • Jerusalem stone (Meleke / Mizzi). The default for our region — warm, breathing, ageing well.
  • Basalt. Northern weight; pairs with green in a way few stones do.
  • Local limestone gravel. Paths that hold their shape through winter.

Metal & wood

  • Oxidised steel (Corten). Used sparingly — for edges, planters, gates. Ages into the soil.
  • Untreated cedar, oak, iroko. Wood that turns silver, not grey.

Principles

  • Plant in groups of odd numbers (3, 5, 8, 13) — the rhythm of natural drift.
  • 70% structural & evergreen, 30% seasonal — gardens earn their keep in winter, not summer.
  • Native first, then naturalised, then ornamental — in that order.
  • Water with the climate, not against it. Drip lines, not sprinklers.
  • Edit every season. The garden of year three is the one worth waiting for.
