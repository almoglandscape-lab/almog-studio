A philosophy of honest gardens.

I'm Almog. I design gardens the way a coral grows — slowly, in one place, in response to the light and the people around it. This is how I think about the work.

Gardens are places, not pictures.

A garden is judged with the body, not the eye. The bare foot on warm stone, the shoulder brushing a shrub, the temperature dropping two degrees as you turn the corner — these are the real materials. I design for the body first and the photograph second.

The site already knows.

Every site has a direction, a sound, a soil. My job is to listen long enough to hear it. The best move is usually the smallest one: opening a view that was always there, lifting a stone that was always under the grass, letting the wind keep the path it had before us.

Honest materials, native plants.

I work mostly with local stone, native Mediterranean planting, oxidised metals and untreated wood. Drought-wise species — rosemary, lavender, salvia, olive, carob, oak — survive and improve our climate. The planting plan is a long letter to the place, not a costume.

Composition, quietly.

Behind every garden I design there is a structure — usually golden ratio (1.618) and a Fibonacci rhythm of spacings. You won't see it. You'll feel it as rightness. The mathematics of how leaves arrange themselves around a stem is the same mathematics I use to arrange a path around a house.

Psychology before plants.

Before a single plant is chosen, I ask: where will the family eat in summer; where does the child want to hide; where does the host want guests to pause. A garden is a small theatre for daily life. Get the choreography right and the planting writes itself.

Patience and passion.

Two words I keep at the studio door. Passion makes a garden begin. Patience makes it last. A good garden is never finished — only edited, season after season. I stay with the gardens I make.
